A field guide for TSA's AI practitioners

__YOU ALREADY KNOW__

You have looked at a process in this building and known — not suspected, known — that it could be done in a fraction of the time with the right approach\.

You have sat in a meeting watching someone manually do something for the fourth hour in a row and thought about what you would build if anyone asked you to\.

You have wondered why the gap between what the tools can do and what the institution actually does with them is so wide\.

You are not imagining it\. The gap is real\. And you are not alone in seeing it\.

__The limitation has never been the stack\. It has been the mental model\.__

TSA's existing environment — Excel, SharePoint, Power Automate, Teams, DHSChat — is more capable than most people in this building have ever tested\. The constraint is not tools\. It is the assumption that transformation requires new tools, new budget, or someone else's permission to start\.

Builders don't wait for those things\. They work with what is in front of them\.

__WHAT BUILDERS BELIEVE__

These are not principles handed down from above\. They are the operating assumptions of people who have already been building — discovered through the work, not before it\.

__01  __The process is the problem, not the person\. If something is slow, broken, or error\-prone, the answer is not to work harder or faster\. The answer is to redesign the workflow\.

__02  __Constraints are credentials\. No Python\. No external infrastructure\. No budget\. These are not apologies — they are proof that the solution is deployable to everyone, not just whoever has special access\.

__03  __Multiplication beats personal productivity\. A workflow you hand off to a colleague does more for the institution than one you run yourself for years\. The goal is never to be indispensable\. The goal is to make the capability normal\.

__04  __Repeatable beats impressive\. A freight train that runs every day is worth more than a Formula One car that runs once\. Build for reliability, not admiration\.

__05  __Start from the human system\. Map the workflow before you touch a tool\. Understand what decision is being made, what inputs it requires, and what the output needs to look like\. The technology fills in last\.

__06  __Encode what you know\. Tribal knowledge that lives in your head is a liability\. Document it\. Structure it\. Make it referenceable\. That is the difference between an expert and an institutional asset\.

__07  __Ship to learn\. A working draft in the hands of a real user teaches more than another week of refinement\. Done and deployable beats perfect and hypothetical\.

__WHAT BUILDERS DO__

Building is not a role\. It is a practice\. It looks like this:

Builders watch processes before they touch them\. They map inputs, decisions, and outputs before writing a single line of code or crafting a single prompt\. The discipline is understanding the problem completely before proposing the solution\.

Builders work in the open\. They share what they build, document how it works, and hand it off before it becomes load\-bearing\. A tool that only one person can run is a risk, not a capability\.

Builders close the loop\. They follow up\. They find out whether what they built actually solved the problem or just solved the problem they imagined\. Then they adjust\.

Builders teach\. The most valuable thing a builder can do is make another builder\. Every colleague who learns to run a workflow independently is a force multiplier — the original builder's impact grows without the original builder having to be present\.

__WHAT THIS IS NOT__

Being clear about what does not belong here is as important as being clear about what does\.

__—  __This is not about being the smartest person in the room\. Builders are not experts performing expertise\. They are practitioners solving problems\.

__—  __This is not about collecting tools\. A builder who chases every new platform or technique without finishing what they started is not building — they are exploring\. Exploration has value, but it is not the same thing\.

__—  __This is not hero work\. Builders do not hoard what they know to make themselves irreplaceable\. The goal is institutional capability, not personal leverage\.

__—  __This is not about waiting for permission\. If you can see the problem and you have access to the tools, the work can begin\. Bureaucratic patience and operational initiative are not mutually exclusive\.

__—  __This is not a title or a designation\. Nobody grants you the identity of builder\. You take it by doing the work\.

__WHAT THIS MOMENT REQUIRES__

TSA is at an early inflection point in its relationship with AI\. The experiments have been run\. The tools work\. The patterns are proven\.

What determines whether this moment compounds into something lasting or fades into a footnote is not technology\. It is people — specifically, whether there are enough people inside this building who understand the practice of building deeply enough to carry it forward, teach it, and adapt it to problems that haven't been named yet\.

That is the gap\. And it is the gap that builders fill\.

__The institution does not change because someone at the top decided it should\. It changes because enough people at every level started doing things differently\.__

You do not need to have built anything yet to belong here\. You need to be the kind of person who, after reading this, is already thinking about what you would build first\.

__THE INVITATION__

If you recognize yourself in this document — if the problems feel familiar and the principles feel obvious — then you are already a builder\. You just may not have had a place to practice yet\.

That is what this community is for\. Not a committee\. Not a working group with a charter and a sponsor and a biweekly meeting cadence\. A network of practitioners who share what they build, teach what they know, and raise the floor for everyone around them\.

The only qualification is the willingness to start\.
